The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is the most abundant gas in Earth's atmosphere, comprising approximately 78% of the air we breathe. Despite its prevalence, most living organisms cannot directly utilize atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) in its diatomic form. The nitrogen cycle describes the series of biological and geochemical processes by which nitrogen is converted between its various chemical forms as it moves through the atmosphere, terrestrial ecosystems, and aquatic environments. Key transformations include nitrogen fixation, carried out by specialized prokaryotes such as Rhizobium residing in the root nodules of leguminous plants; nitrification, in which ammonium ions are oxidized first to nitrite and then to nitrate by soil bacteria including Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter; assimilation, through which plants and fungi absorb inorganic nitrogen for biosynthesis of amino acids and nucleic acids; ammonification, the decomposition of organic nitrogen back to ammonia by saprotrophic microorganisms; and denitrification, by which anaerobic bacteria return nitrogen gas to the atmosphere. Human activities — particularly the widespread application of synthetic fertilizers derived from the Haber-Bosch process and the combustion of fossil fuels — have substantially altered the natural nitrogen cycle, contributing to environmental consequences such as eutrophication of water bodies, hypoxic dead zones, and the production of nitrous oxide, a potent greenhouse gas.

Understanding the nitrogen cycle is fundamental to agronomy, ecology, and climate science. Sustainable agricultural practices, including crop rotation with legumes and precision fertilizer management, aim to minimize nitrogen losses while maintaining soil fertility. Ongoing research into biological nitrogen fixation and nitrification inhibitors holds promise for reducing the environmental footprint of global food production without compromising crop yields.
